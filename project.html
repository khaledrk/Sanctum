02 / Project

RUINS REGROUNDED

Shornemead Fort and the Thames marshes are treated as a changing civic and ecological ground rather than a preserved object.

SITE

Estuarine ruins

The project follows a landscape shaped by defence, extraction, abandonment, flood, informal access and ecological succession. The fort is not isolated from the marsh; both are part of a larger gradient of active ruination.

TRANSECT

From dock to ruined fort

A territorial transect traces repair, fabrication, industry, grazing, flooding, occupation and ecological succession. Ruination appears differently at every point along the route.

TWELVE OPENINGS

Access through subtraction

The proposal uses twelve cuts and thresholds to move the fort from enclosure toward shared ground. Each opening responds to a different material condition, route, level and relationship with water.

01–03

Existing breach

Record and stabilise informal openings already made through occupation and weathering.

04–06

Tidal threshold

Allow water, sediment and wetland species to enter selected chambers.

07–09

Shared route

Connect the fort to existing walking patterns without turning it into a controlled heritage object.

10–12

Incomplete room

Retain voids, fragments and partially occupied interiors as spaces for gathering, observation and seasonal use.

REGROUNDING

A floodscape of future ruins

Rather than defend every edge, the proposal identifies where flooding, active ruination and long-term occupation can coexist. New ground is formed through sediment, planting, reuse and phased access.

OUTCOME

Not restored. Not abandoned.

The fort remains unfinished, accessible and continually remade. The project proposes a landscape capable of holding memory, ecological change and public life without resolving them into a single image.
